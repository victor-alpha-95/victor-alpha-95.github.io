Building With Everything

June 2026 · 0→1 inside a giant · Builders & operators

Inside a large company the resources are all there. The trouble is they already belong to someone — and the real skill is turning power on paper into power that’s committed to your thing.

Startups teach you to build with nothing. A large company teaches you to build with everything. Both are hard. They’re just not hard in the same way, and the second kind tends to catch people off guard.

The startup story is the one everybody romanticizes. Two people, a garage, no money, no brand. You make something from scraps because scraps are all there is. The constraint is at least legible: there isn’t enough, and every “no” comes from the universe. But legible is not the same as gentle. The market doesn’t want it yet, the runway is short, and the bank balance is honest with you every single morning, whether you want the truth or not.

Now look at the other side of it: building something new inside a large company. From the outside it looks like easy mode. There’s money, engineers, a balance sheet, a brand people already trust, and distribution that took decades to build. Every ingredient a founder would kill for is sitting right there in the building.

And that is exactly the trap.

The resources are real. They’re just not yours.

In a startup, scarcity is the enemy and resourcefulness is the cure. It’s punishing, but at least you can see the fight you’re in. Inside a giant, the enemy inverts and goes quiet. The fight isn’t a lack of resources. It’s that all of them already belong to someone, are already committed to something, and were allocated long before you walked in with your idea. The money has an owner. The engineers have a roadmap. The brand has guardians whose entire job is to keep people like you from touching it.

Clayton Christensen had a clean way to cut this. A company, he argued, is three things: its resources, its processes, and its priorities. Resources are the part everyone counts: the cash, the people, the patents. But what a company can actually build is decided by its processes and its priorities, and those tend to be set against the new thing by design. A giant’s processes exist to repeat what already works. Its priorities are tuned to protect the core. A 0→1 idea is, almost by definition, the thing the system was built to filter out.

Which may be why the companies that have pulled it off so often did so by stepping outside their own machinery. When IBM set out to build a personal computer in 1980, it didn’t route the project through the company’s normal development process; it stood up a small, deliberately insulated team in Boca Raton, Florida, free to use off-the-shelf parts and skip the usual sign-offs, and shipped the IBM PC about a year later, in August 1981. Lockheed’s Skunk Works ran the same way for decades, a separate shop with its own rules, kept at arm’s length from the corporate bureaucracy precisely so it could move. Christensen’s own prescription was close to that: when the core’s processes and priorities will smother the new thing, give it its own.

But most people building inside a giant never get a Boca Raton. They have to win that freedom the hard way, one yes at a time. So the skill that compounds there may not be resourcefulness at all. It looks more like something you could call ‘conversion’ — turning power that exists on paper into power that’s actually committed to your thing. A founder earns resources. A corporate builder earns permission. They’re different muscles, and the second one is chronically underrated. From the outside it looks like maneuvering; from the inside, it’s trust.

And the currency you spend to do it isn’t budget. It’s belief. An engineer gets seconded to you because a director decided to bet a little of her own credibility on you. The brand team says yes to an experiment because they’ve watched you not break things before. Almost every resource you mobilize inside a giant is really a person choosing to lend you something they’re accountable for. The org chart says you have authority. The work happens on relationships.

Which is why the two teach so differently. Scarcity tells you the truth fast, and to your face. Abundance is quieter about it. It shows you a warehouse full of everything you need and lets you believe the hard part is over, right until you try to move a single crate and find it bolted to the floor, with someone else holding the key.

It may be part of why so many brilliant founders seem to struggle when they join a big company, and why so many strong operators appear to flounder when they leave for a startup. Perhaps each has trained one muscle and then walked into a gym that only has the other. The founder who could conjure something from nothing can suddenly find it hard to get three people aligned; the operator who could move a ten-thousand-person organization is left with no organization to move, and no one to persuade but reality.

If you’re building inside a giant right now and it feels slow, frustrating, faintly absurd, like you’re surrounded by everything you need and can’t reach any of it, that feeling may be the actual job. It isn’t necessarily a sign you’re in the wrong place. It might be the curriculum.

The patience it asks for is the patience of any long, unglamorous coast where compounding does its quiet work: the throughline of Plateaus Are the Point. Permission compounds the way trust does. Slowly, then suddenly.

Build with nothing and you learn how much you can do alone. Build with everything and you learn the other half: how little of what matters was ever yours to command, and how much of the job is convincing the people who hold the rest.

What’s the resource you keep assuming you’ll get? And have you actually earned the yes for it yet?
