Plateaus Are the Point

June 2026 · A spacecraft, six years on · Self-mastery, compounding

In a culture that worships the launch, the people who actually arrive are the ones who learn to love the coast.

Six years ago, as an MBA student trying to out-run my own impatience, I wrote a piece comparing self-growth to a spacecraft leaving Earth. The argument was simple. The violent thrust that gets you off the ground is the wrong fuel for the journey that follows. Burn it too long and you flame out before you ever reach the ‘galaxies afar’. At some point you have to cut the thrusters and switch to something slower.

I believed it then. I just hadn’t lived it yet.

In the years since, I’ve watched that same curve play out. Not in a self-improvement diary, but in businesses. I’ve sat in rooms where a brand-new venture grows 0→1 on sheer momentum and then, right on cue, stalls. I’ve seen teams sprint brilliantly for two quarters and then plateau, and I’ve watched leaders respond the way I once responded to mine: by firing the thrusters again. More effort. More hours. More intensity. And, just as predictably, more burnout. Of people, of budgets, of goodwill.

So I want to revisit the analogy, because I think I got the shape right and the meaning wrong.

The part I missed

When I first drew the spacecraft, I treated the plateau as a problem: a flat, frustrating stretch to be survived on the way to somewhere better. That framing is exactly backwards. In the actual physics of the thing, the long coast between the launch and the destination isn’t the boring part of the trip. It is most of the trip. It’s where the spacecraft covers the greatest distance on the least fuel. The thrust is loud and visible and lasts a few minutes. The coast is quiet and invisible and lasts for years, and it’s the coast that actually gets you there.

The plateau, in other words, is not a pause in your progress. The plateau is the progress.

The analysis, rewritten

Here’s the mapping I’d offer my 24-year-old self now.

The ‘thrusters’ (that early, irrational burst of effort) are how you escape gravity: a new skill, a new role. The visible gains come fast, and they fool you into believing the whole curve will be that steep.

The ‘plateau’ is what happens next, and it feels like failure because nothing is visibly moving. But this is precisely where mastery compounds. Reps accumulate. Judgment quietly sharpens. Relationships deepen below the surface. An edge gets built. None of it shows up on the dashboard this week, and all of it is the actual asset.

The ‘mistake’ is reading flat as failure and re-firing the thrusters. In a career, that looks like jumping roles every time the dopamine fades. In a business, it looks like chasing a new initiative the moment the current one stops spiking. Both burn the one fuel you can’t refuel on the move: your own sustained attention.

The ‘move’ is to trust the coast. Course-correct on direction, not speed. And account for the ‘external factors’, the line from my original essay I’d now underline twice: the family, the health, the relationships that aren’t a distraction from the journey but the reason there’s a journey at all.

What I actually believe now

We live in a culture that worships the launch. The promotion, the raise, the funding round, the viral moment: all thrust, all spectacle. Almost nobody posts about the coast, because the coast doesn’t photograph well. But the people who actually arrive somewhere are, almost without exception, the ones who learned to love the boring middle. The ones who stayed long enough in one orbit for compounding to do its slow, unglamorous work.

If there’s a single thing I’d tell anyone standing on a plateau and itching to hit the throttle, it’s this: you are not stuck. You are cruising. Hold your direction, protect your fuel, and let the distance accumulate.

The galaxies afar were never reached in a sprint.

What plateau are you mistaking for a dead end right now?
