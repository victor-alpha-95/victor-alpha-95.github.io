I Set Out to Build a Website. I Built a System Instead.

June 2026 · Build notes · AI-native, systems thinking

I wanted one page that said who I am. I ended up with a small system that runs itself.

I wanted a simple thing: one page on the internet that said who I am and linked my profiles. A weekend job. What I ended up with runs itself, and the distance between those two outcomes is the whole point of this piece.

The brief stayed small for about an hour. Then a better question surfaced. Should this be a page, or a process? A page is a noun. It sits there and slowly goes stale. What I actually wanted was a verb: something that keeps working after I walk away.

So we built for that. The site still looks like a quiet, minimal page. Underneath, it keeps itself current and surfaces me only at the moments that genuinely call for a human judgment rather than a mechanical one. I don’t log in, and I don’t maintain it. I approve things, and that is nearly the whole of my job here.

Since then it has grown a few more limbs. It measures itself now, so I can see what actually lands. It hands each finished piece to the right shape for each platform. And it gives readers a couple of quiet ways to stick around, by email or on a WhatsApp channel, so an audience can compound the same way the writing does. That last detail is the one I keep turning over, because it’s really a lesson about delegation, which is the skill I spend most of my working life trying to get better at.

Good delegation isn’t offloading a task. It’s handing over an outcome while keeping the judgment for yourself. Every part of this project works that way. The machine carries the heavy, boring middle, and it stops at the single moment that matters: the decision to put my name on something. Nothing goes out under my name that I haven’t said yes to, and that one rule is what makes the whole thing safe to trust.

It also changed what I was willing to aim for. I didn’t want a site that merely exists. I wanted one that compounds, that gets a little more useful each week without me touching it, so the effort I spent once keeps paying out long after I’ve forgotten I spent it.

There’s an honest catch, and it’s worth naming. None of this takes me out of the loop, and it shouldn’t. The taste, the judgment, the choice to publish: those stay mine. The machine is fast and tireless and a little flat. I’m slower, more limited, and the one who actually cares, which is exactly why the trade that works is to let the machine carry the load while I keep the wheel.

So here’s the reframe I’d offer anyone about to build their own small, fixed thing. Ask whether it should be a ‘noun’ or a ‘verb’. A noun sits there. A verb keeps working after you leave. Almost everything worth building is quietly the second kind.

This site is the proof of that. It even helped write the sentence you’re reading.
